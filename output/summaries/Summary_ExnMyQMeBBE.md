## Overview
This Mata Najwa episode featured a debate on Indonesia's proposed Criminal Procedure Code (RKUHAP), focusing on concerns that the draft expands police powers without adequate safeguards. Deputy Justice Minister Prof. Eddy Hiariej defended the draft against criticism from civil society advocates led by Arief Azrael (LokaTaru) and Medina Rahmawati (Civil Society Coalition), with Golkar parliamentarian Sudesan Tandra moderating the discussion. The program highlighted specific cases of police abuse, including the death of Ernawati's brother in custody and her subsequent imprisonment under the ITE Law for speaking out, illustrating the urgent need for procedural reforms to protect citizens from arbitrary law enforcement.

## Key Highlights
The debate centered on whether the draft RKUHAP strengthens or weakens citizen protections. Civil society critics argued that the legislation normalizes existing police abuses by codifying practices like restorative justice at the investigation stage, where no crime has yet been established. They pointed to specific concerns including the absence of habeas corpus protections, expanded investigative powers for police (including undercover operations), and provisions allowing police to bypass judicial authorization for detention in subjectively-defined "urgent situations." The critics emphasized that current safeguards are inadequate, noting that the draft lacks mandatory judicial review within 48 hours of arrest, a basic protection recommended by international human rights standards.

Professor Hiariej countered that the draft actually constrains police powers by requiring judicial approval for seven of nine coercive measures and mandating lawyer presence from the investigation stage onward. He argued that transparency mechanisms, including electronic case tracking systems, would prevent cases from stalling indefinitely. However, civil society advocates challenged this, noting that many proposed safeguards contained exceptions that could negate their effectiveness. The debate revealed fundamental disagreements about whether the reform addresses systemic problems or merely legitimizes current practices that often result in human rights violations.

The program featured a particularly powerful segment with Ernawati, whose brother died in police custody within three hours of arrest. Her subsequent imprisonment for criticizing police handling of the case under the ITE Law exemplified the chilling effect on those who seek accountability. Her testimony, calling for transparency and proper investigation procedures, underscored the human cost of inadequate legal protections and the urgent need for meaningful reforms rather than cosmetic changes to existing procedures.

## Implications
This debate reflects broader tensions between security imperatives and human rights protections in Indonesia's criminal justice reform. The rushed legislative process, with limited public input on such fundamental legal changes, risks reproducing past patterns where reform efforts fail to address systemic abuses. Given that this legislation will govern criminal procedures for decades, the outcome will significantly impact Indonesia's rule of law and democratic governance.
