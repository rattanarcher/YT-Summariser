## Overview
This episode of Mata Najwa featured a comprehensive debate on Indonesia's Criminal Procedure Code Bill (RUU KUHAP), involving Deputy Law Minister Prof. Edy Hiraish, DPR member Sudeson Tandra from Golkar Party, advocate Arief Azrael from Lokatharu, and civil society coalition member Medina Rahmawati. The discussion centered on concerns that the draft law expands police powers without adequate safeguards, potentially legitimizing current abusive practices rather than reforming them. Key issues included the lack of meaningful public participation in the legislative process, insufficient protection against police brutality during arrests, and controversial provisions around restorative justice that could enable case dismissals without proper oversight.

## Key Highlights
The debate revealed fundamental disagreements about the bill's impact on police powers. Civil society representatives argued that rather than creating checks and balances, the draft normalizes existing problematic practices. They highlighted that the bill allows police to conduct various investigative actions during the preliminary investigation phase, including undercover operations and controlled deliveries, which significantly expands their authority beyond simply confirming whether a crime occurred. The restorative justice provisions were particularly contentious, with advocates citing cases where victims were pressured to accept settlements under threat of counter-charges.

The legislative process itself came under heavy criticism for being rushed and non-participatory. Civil society groups complained that despite early consultations, their recommendations were largely ignored, with less than 10% of crucial proposals being incorporated. The draft mysteriously appeared in parliamentary sessions without clear documentation of how public input was processed. Government representatives defended the process, claiming over 50% of civil society input was accommodated, but this was strongly disputed by the advocates present.

Real-world consequences of inadequate police oversight were dramatically illustrated through the case of Ernawati, who was imprisoned under cybercrime laws after reporting police brutality that allegedly killed her brother. Her case exemplified the "no viral, no justice" phenomenon plaguing Indonesian law enforcement, where police misconduct only receives attention when it becomes a social media sensation. The discussion revealed systemic problems with case reporting and follow-up, with hundreds of thousands of police reports going unprocessed annually.

## Implications
This debate reflects deeper tensions in Indonesian criminal justice reform, where expanding state investigative powers conflicts with protecting individual rights. The rushed timeline to complete the bill before the new Criminal Code takes effect in January 2026 raises concerns about proper legislative scrutiny. The case highlights Indonesia's ongoing struggle to create effective oversight mechanisms for law enforcement while maintaining operational effectiveness in crime prevention.
