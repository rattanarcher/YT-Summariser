## Overview

Aceh Governor Mulia Novizal speaks with Najwa Shihab about the devastating floods that struck Aceh province, which he describes as "the second tsunami." The interview, conducted 10 days into the disaster response, covers the unprecedented nature of the flooding, challenges in relief distribution, criticism of local officials' response, and the need for continued assistance. The governor details how the floods affected 18 regencies and cities across Aceh, with some areas still completely cut off from aid, while also defending his decision to seek international assistance directly from friendly nations.

## Key Highlights

The floods that hit Aceh have created destruction comparable to the 2004 tsunami, according to Governor Novizal, who describes disturbing phenomena during the eight-day deluge. Unlike the tsunami which lasted 20 minutes, these floods produced mysterious black water that caused itching and burning sensations, along with foul odors. Bodies couldn't be buried for 2-3 days and had to be tied to trees or bridges due to flooded burial grounds. Even large animals like snakes and monitor lizards that normally survive natural disasters were found dead, while wooden houses were completely swept away and mixed with thick mud reaching waist-deep levels. The governor emphasizes the unusual nature of these floods compared to previous natural disasters in the region.

Recovery efforts have shown mixed progress, with basic infrastructure like electricity and smaller bridges being restored, allowing inter-district movement to resume. However, four regencies remain largely inaccessible by land, requiring helicopter and boat access for relief distribution. The governor specifically requested additional helicopters from President Jokowi, receiving two units that have helped reach isolated areas, though many remote locations in East Aceh remain untouched with residents still crying out for basic supplies. International assistance has been accepted directly from friendly nations including Malaysia and China, who have provided medical supplies and personnel, with Chinese medical teams working for three days and scheduled to return.

The governor issued sharp criticism against local district heads (bupati) whom he accused of abandoning their responsibilities during the crisis, with one reportedly seen at a karaoke venue instead of helping flood victims. He emphasized that these officials were elected by the people and should not show indifference or incompetence during disasters. The most severely affected areas include Tamiang, North Aceh, East Aceh, and Tekingan regencies, with immediate needs including medicines, cooking equipment, religious supplies, blankets, clothing, and LPG gas cylinders. The governor also highlighted concerns about children's education, as many schools were destroyed and teachers cannot reach remote affected areas.

## Implications

This disaster represents a significant test for both local and national government disaster response capabilities in Indonesia, particularly highlighting coordination challenges between different levels of government. The governor's decision to seek direct international assistance and his public criticism of local officials signals potential tensions in Indonesia's multi-level governance structure during crisis management.
