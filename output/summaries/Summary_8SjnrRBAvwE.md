## Overview
This video from Tempo's "Bocor Alus Politik" examines the stalled deliberations over Indonesia's electoral law revision in the Indonesian Parliament (DPR). The discussion reveals significant tensions within President Prabowo's coalition regarding key electoral reforms, particularly the parliamentary threshold that determines which parties can secure legislative seats. The hosts analyze internal documents from closed Commission 2 meetings and interviews with politicians, showing how disagreements over electoral rules could potentially fracture the governing coalition.

## Key Highlights

The parliamentary threshold issue has emerged as the most contentious aspect of electoral law revision. The Constitutional Court previously ruled the current 4% threshold unconstitutional, forcing lawmakers to develop new formulas. Three options are being considered: maintaining 4% (impossible due to the court ruling), increasing to 5%, or decreasing to 3.5%. The 5% option particularly concerns coalition parties because it would apply to regional legislatures (DPRD) for the first time, potentially eliminating smaller parties entirely from local politics. Politicians fear that parties unable to reach the threshold would lose all representation from national to local levels, fundamentally altering Indonesia's political landscape.

The electoral system debate between open and closed proportional representation continues to divide the coalition. Under the current open system, voters select individual candidates, while a closed system would only allow voting for party symbols. PDIP has long advocated for the closed system, but other coalition members worry this would benefit parties with strong national recognition over those dependent on local figures. The timeline concerns are equally problematic - politicians suggest deliberately delaying passage until close to deadlines to prevent Constitutional Court challenges, ensuring any ruling would come after the 2029 elections.

## Implications

These internal disagreements highlight the fragility of Prabowo's coalition when party survival interests override collective governance. The potential use of emergency presidential decrees (PERPU) if legislative deadlock continues could undermine democratic processes and sideline parliamentary input on crucial electoral reforms.
