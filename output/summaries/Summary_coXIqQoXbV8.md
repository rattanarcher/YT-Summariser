## Overview
Political commentator Akbar Faizal delivers a critical analysis of the role of violence in Indonesian political discourse, arguing that certain groups view violence as the most effective form of communication to advance their agendas. He examines the mindset of those who resort to violence against critics and activists, suggesting they operate under a doctrine that discourages dissent and opposition. The commentary appears to address recent incidents of political violence while exploring the psychological and institutional factors that enable such behavior to persist without consequences.

## Key Highlights

Faizal begins by expressing disappointment that some parties still believe that harming democracy is the best path to winning their agenda, firmly stating that such approaches will never succeed. He identifies violence as what these groups consider the most effective language of communication, suggesting they have become accustomed to solving problems through force. According to his analysis, these actors fundamentally reject the existence of critical activists in the country, believing instead that the nation should receive total support from all citizens without any differences of opinion or opposition.

The commentary delves into the psychological conditioning of those who commit political violence, suggesting they may lack personal autonomy in choosing whether to carry out or cancel attacks. Instead, Faizal argues they are motivated by doctrine or orders that enable them to act with full conviction that their actions are justified. He points to a culture of impunity, where perpetrators believe they will face no punishment because they may have acted similarly before or have observed previous cases going unpunished.

## Implications

This analysis highlights ongoing concerns about the erosion of democratic norms and the normalization of violence against political opposition in Indonesia. The commentary suggests systemic failures in accountability that may be emboldening further attacks on civil society and democratic institutions.
