## Overview

Former Trade Minister Tom Lembong discussed his recent abolition from corruption charges related to sugar imports, granted by President Prabowo Subianto. In an extensive interview with Najwa Shihab, Lembong explained the legal distinction between abolition (acknowledging no crime occurred) versus amnesty (forgiving a crime), and reflected on his relationships with both President Jokowi and President Prabowo. He emphasized his commitment to reconciliation while maintaining his right to speak truth to power, and detailed how his nine months in detention fundamentally changed his worldview about Indonesia's legal system and social inequality.

## Key Highlights

Lembong provided detailed legal reasoning for why he received abolition rather than amnesty, explaining that because he had filed an appeal, the case legally returned to "zero-zero" status, making abolition the appropriate remedy. He argued that abolition acknowledges no crime occurred, whereas amnesty would imply guilt requiring forgiveness. This technical distinction became central to his defense of the presidential decision, as he maintained the case was politically motivated from the beginning and warranted a political solution through executive and legislative branches correcting the judiciary.

The former minister addressed concerns about his relationship with former President Jokowi, acknowledging that while their political paths diverged during the 2024 campaign when he joined Anies Baswedan's team, he remains grateful for the opportunities Jokowi provided him. Lembong admitted his earlier public statements about having "many regrets" from his government service were misinterpreted, clarifying he was referring to policy failures like the shrinking middle class rather than personal regrets about serving. He described their relationship as remaining cordial until he formally joined the opposition campaign, emphasizing his enduring gratitude despite their political differences.

Regarding the abolition's implications for other defendants in the sugar import case, Lembong argued that logic dictates their charges should also be dropped since they face identical accusations. He criticized prosecutors and judges for claiming the abolition only applies to him personally, arguing this approach would essentially admit crimes occurred but create selective enforcement based on political connections. This stance reflects his broader concern about Indonesia's justice system, which he now views as fundamentally flawed and requiring comprehensive reform rather than just economic development.

## Implications

This case represents a significant test of President Prabowo's reconciliation agenda and his willingness to differentiate his administration from his predecessor's approach to political opponents. Lembong's experience highlights systemic problems in Indonesia's legal system that could undermine economic development if left unaddressed, while his emphasis on maintaining critical voices despite receiving government clemency may influence how other political figures navigate the new administration's overtures for unity.
