## Overview

Former Trade Minister Tom Lembong appeared on Mata Najwa to discuss his recent abolition by President Prabowo, which ended his corruption case related to sugar imports during his tenure as trade minister. Lembong explained the legal distinction between abolition and amnesty, defended the decision as politically necessary given the case's political motivations, and positioned it within President Prabowo's broader reconciliation agenda. He emphasized that the abolition came without conditions and expressed gratitude to President Prabowo while maintaining his commitment to continue advocating for truth and justice.

## Key Highlights

Lembong provided detailed legal reasoning for why he received abolition rather than amnesty, explaining that because his team had filed an appeal, the case was legally reset to "zero-zero" between prosecutor and defendant, making abolition the appropriate legal mechanism. He argued this formally recognizes there was no case to begin with, rather than pardoning a crime. He noted the rarity of abolition in Indonesian history and described it as an extraordinary correction by the executive and legislative branches against the judiciary. Lembong acknowledged public disappointment that the case didn't proceed through normal judicial channels but justified accepting abolition due to the unreliability of Indonesia's legal system and the personal toll of continued imprisonment.

The former minister positioned his case within President Prabowo's reconciliation narrative, noting how his case unusually united supporters across political camps (referring to the 01, 02, and 03 presidential candidate camps from the 2024 election). He emphasized that while reconciliation is important, it shouldn't prevent continued criticism and truth-telling. Lembong expressed concern about the remaining ten defendants in the sugar import case, arguing that if his abolition declares there was no case, then logically four of their five charges should also be dropped since they mirror his charges. He criticized any interpretation that would treat his abolition as special immunity rather than recognition of innocence.

## Implications

This case represents a significant test of President Prabowo's reconciliation approach and the independence of Indonesia's judiciary. Lembong's abolition, combined with the amnesty for PDI-P Secretary-General Hasto Kristiyanto, signals the new administration's willingness to use executive power to resolve politically-charged legal cases. The handling of the remaining defendants will be crucial for determining whether this represents genuine legal reform or selective political intervention.
