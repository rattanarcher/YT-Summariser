## Overview

This video discusses the detention of four Indonesian military intelligence (BAIS) personnel suspected of involvement in an acid attack against human rights activist Andri Yunus from Kontras. Former BAIS chief Soleman Ponto strongly condemned the actions while human rights advocate Usman Hamid questioned the command structure behind the attack. The case has raised serious questions about military intelligence overreach and the misuse of state security apparatus against civilians. Both guests emphasized that such actions fall completely outside BAIS's legitimate mandate, which focuses on external threats rather than domestic surveillance.

## Key Highlights

The discussion revealed significant concerns about institutional accountability within Indonesia's military intelligence structure. Soleman Ponto, speaking as a former BAIS commander, expressed shock and condemnation at the involvement of military personnel in what he described as a complete betrayal of BAIS's constitutional function. He emphasized that BAIS is designed to counter external threats and foreign sabotage, not to monitor or attack Indonesian citizens. Ponto suggested the four detained personnel were likely seconded officers (BKO) rather than permanent BAIS members, working in the Denmark unit rather than the main directorates, which could explain their weaker institutional loyalty and oversight.

Usman Hamid from Amnesty International Indonesia raised critical questions about command responsibility, arguing that junior officers like captains and lieutenants would not act independently in such operations. He called for a joint fact-finding team and insisted the case should be tried in civilian courts rather than military tribunals, citing Indonesian law that requires military personnel who commit common crimes against civilians to face civilian justice. Hamid expressed concern that military authorities might attempt to limit accountability to the direct perpetrators while protecting higher-level commanders who may have issued orders.

Both experts agreed on the need for concurrent jurisdiction (koneksitas) allowing both police and military police to investigate simultaneously, with the case ultimately proceeding through civilian courts given that the victim was a civilian. They emphasized that the attack has damaged the reputation of Indonesian military intelligence and called for full transparency in determining who authorized the operation and how high the command responsibility extends.

## Implications

This case represents a significant test of civilian oversight over military institutions in Indonesia and could set important precedents for how military personnel are held accountable for crimes against civilians. The involvement of intelligence officers in attacking a human rights activist raises broader concerns about democratic governance and the protection of civil society in Indonesia.
