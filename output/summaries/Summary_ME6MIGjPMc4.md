## Overview

In this exclusive interview with Bill Gates at the Goalkeepers 2025 forum in New York, Najwa Shihab explored critical global health issues affecting Indonesia, including President Prabowo's flagship free school meals program and Indonesia's participation in tuberculosis vaccine trials. Gates discussed the concerning reversal of child mortality progress globally, emphasized the importance of prioritizing early childhood nutrition over school meal programs, and addressed conspiracy theories surrounding TB vaccine trials in Indonesia. The conversation highlighted Indonesia's evolving role from aid recipient to global solution provider in health initiatives.

## Key Highlights

Gates delivered a stark assessment of President Prabowo's ambitious free school meals program, arguing that while well-intentioned, it misses the critical window for addressing malnutrition. He emphasized that the key to combating stunting lies in the first thousand days from pregnancy through age two, stating that "the key to nutrition is really very early in life" and that "after you pass those early years, you cannot catch up." This perspective challenges the prioritization of school feeding programs when resources could be better allocated to maternal and infant nutrition interventions during the crucial early development period.

The discussion revealed Indonesia's significant participation in groundbreaking tuberculosis vaccine trials, marking the first new TB vaccine development in over 50 years. With TB killing over one million people globally annually and 134,000 in Indonesia alone, Gates defended the trials against conspiracy theories and misinformation campaigns. He emphasized that these humanitarian efforts, funded by $150 million in grants to Indonesia, aim to combat a "disease of the poor" that disproportionately affects the most vulnerable populations. The trials represent not just participation in global health research but also Indonesia's path toward pharmaceutical independence through partnerships with local producers like Bio Farma.

Gates warned about the dangerous spread of health misinformation, particularly conspiracy theories targeting vaccine programs, calling it "one of the biggest risk factors for global health." He noted how anti-vaccine groups manipulate statements from global figures to build false narratives about population control and Western dominance. Meanwhile, he announced his commitment to donate all his resources over the next 20 years to global health causes, while acknowledging that international aid is declining precisely when scientific breakthroughs offer unprecedented opportunities to save lives.

## Implications

This conversation underscores Indonesia's transition from aid recipient to global health partner, with implications for how middle-income countries balance domestic health priorities with international collaboration. The debate over resource allocation between popular political programs like free school meals versus evidence-based early childhood interventions reflects broader challenges in health policy prioritization across developing nations.
