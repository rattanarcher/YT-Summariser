## Overview

This video features a heated debate between political scientist Professor Saiful Mujani and political analyst Iwan Piliang, mediated by host Akbar Faizal, regarding calls to remove President Prabowo Subianto from office. Professor Mujani argues that Prabowo poses a fundamental threat to Indonesian democracy by expressing preferences for the original 1945 Constitution and displaying authoritarian tendencies. Piliang counters that Prabowo's economic policies demonstrate his commitment to the people and that there is insufficient legal basis for impeachment. The discussion reveals deep divisions over Indonesia's democratic trajectory and the appropriate mechanisms for holding leaders accountable.

## Key Highlights

Professor Saiful Mujani presents a systematic case against President Prabowo, arguing that the source of Indonesia's current political turmoil lies with the president himself. He contends that Prabowo's stated preference for returning to the original 1945 Constitution represents a direct threat to Indonesia's democratic reforms achieved through the post-Suharto amendments. Mujani emphasizes that the original constitution lacked democratic safeguards, with the president not directly elected by the people and no term limits. He views Prabowo's constitutional preferences as evidence of authoritarian inclinations similar to those seen in other democracies worldwide where elected leaders later undermined democratic institutions. Beyond constitutional concerns, Mujani points to specific violations, including the appointment of active military personnel to civilian positions and unauthorized cooperation agreements with Israel, as evidence that Prabowo lacks commitment to constitutional governance.

Iwan Piliang offers a vigorous defense of the president, arguing that Prabowo's policies demonstrate genuine commitment to economic populism and addressing systemic corruption. He highlights the president's efforts to reclaim illegally held plantation lands, combat transfer pricing schemes that cost the state trillions in revenue, and improve basic education and nutrition programs. Piliang contends that criticisms of Prabowo's constitutional views are outdated and fail to recognize that people can change over time. He argues that the president's openness to dialogue with media and civil society figures, his extensive reading habits, and his focus on accelerating development programs demonstrate leadership qualities rather than authoritarianism. Piliang maintains that over 80% of the population supports Prabowo's policies, suggesting that calls for his removal are disconnected from popular sentiment.

The debate reveals fundamental disagreements about democratic governance in Indonesia. While Mujani emphasizes constitutional procedures and institutional safeguards as essential to democracy, Piliang focuses on policy outcomes and popular support as measures of legitimate leadership. Both acknowledge existing problems within the government but propose different solutions - Mujani calls for public pressure to force accountability, while Piliang advocates for continued dialogue and patience with ongoing reforms. The discussion illustrates broader tensions in Indonesian politics between procedural democracy and populist governance approaches.

## Implications

This debate reflects deeper anxieties about Indonesia's democratic consolidation 26 years after the fall of Suharto. The sharp disagreement between two prominent intellectuals signals potential polarization within civil society over fundamental questions of governance and accountability. The discussion also highlights ongoing challenges in balancing executive power with institutional checks, particularly regarding military involvement in civilian affairs and constitutional interpretation.
