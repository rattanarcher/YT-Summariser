## Overview

Najwa Shihab's analysis examines the 79th UN General Assembly, focusing on the intersection of street protests and diplomatic discourse around Gaza, particularly Netanyahu's controversial speech and President Prabowo's landmark address proposing Indonesia's potential recognition of Israel. The episode captures the tension between diplomatic rhetoric and grassroots activism, exploring how Indonesia navigates between its traditional pro-Palestine stance and emerging diplomatic pragmatism on the global stage.

## Key Highlights

The episode opens with dramatic scenes from New York, where thousands of pro-Palestine demonstrators marched from Times Square to UN headquarters as Israeli Prime Minister Netanyahu delivered his speech inside. These protesters, organized by coalitions like "Shut It Down for Palestine," represented a growing American movement that has sustained momentum since October 2023. The demonstrations created what Najwa describes as "two different stages" - the official diplomatic podium inside the UN and the passionate voices on the streets outside, with the latter proving more audible and emotionally resonant.

President Prabowo Subianto's maiden UN speech became the episode's central diplomatic focus, marking a potential shift in Indonesia's Middle East policy. For the first time, an Indonesian president explicitly suggested the possibility of recognizing Israel as part of a two-state solution, offering security guarantees to Israel while maintaining support for Palestinian independence. This balanced approach drew sharp attention from Netanyahu himself, who specifically mentioned Indonesia as the world's largest Muslim nation during his own address. Prabowo's position sparked intense domestic debate, with critics arguing he weakened Indonesia's historical solidarity with Palestine, while supporters viewed it as strategic diplomatic realism that could enhance Jakarta's global influence.

The episode also explores the broader wave of Palestinian recognition by Western nations including Australia, Canada, and the UK, reflecting shifting geopolitical dynamics. Australian Prime Minister Anthony Albanese explained his country's recognition as overdue, emphasizing that the 1948 UN partition plan originally envisioned two states. However, street-level activists interviewed by Najwa expressed deep skepticism about these diplomatic gestures, dismissing them as "meaningless symbols" that fail to translate into concrete action to stop what they term ongoing genocide in Gaza.

## Implications

This coverage highlights Indonesia's evolving role in Middle East diplomacy, suggesting Jakarta may be positioning itself as a pragmatic mediator rather than maintaining its traditional uncompromising pro-Palestine stance. The episode also underscores growing questions about the UN's relevance and effectiveness, as both world leaders and street protesters express frustration with the organization's inability to translate diplomatic rhetoric into meaningful action on urgent global crises.
