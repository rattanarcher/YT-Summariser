## Overview

This episode of Mata Najwa examines the contrasting scenes at the UN General Assembly during Gaza debates, featuring President Prabowo Subianto's controversial speech that suggested potential Indonesian recognition of Israel as part of a two-state solution. The program captures the dramatic tension between Netanyahu's address inside the UN hall while thousands of pro-Palestine protesters demonstrated outside, alongside growing international recognition of Palestinian statehood by countries like Australia, Canada, and the UK. Host Najwa Shihab explores Indonesia's shifting diplomatic stance, Trump's 20-point Gaza peace plan, and mounting criticism of the UN's effectiveness in resolving global conflicts.

## Key Highlights

The episode reveals a stark divide between diplomatic rhetoric and street activism during the UN General Assembly's Gaza discussions. Inside the hall, Netanyahu delivered a defiant speech defending Israel's actions while several delegations staged walkouts in protest. Outside, thousands of demonstrators from across the United States, organized by coalitions like "We Sit Down for Palestine," demanded Netanyahu's arrest and called for an end to what they termed genocide in Gaza. The protesters, ranging from young activists to elderly supporters, represented a growing American sentiment that views Israel's actions as inhumane and believes the Palestinian people deserve self-determination.

President Prabowo's UN debut generated significant controversy with his suggestion that Indonesia might recognize Israel as part of achieving Palestinian independence through a two-state solution. This marked a potential shift from Indonesia's historical position of unconditional support for Palestine without Israeli recognition. Prabowo emphasized that both descendants of Abraham should live in peace and security, while acknowledging the suffering in Gaza. His comments drew mixed reactions domestically, with critics viewing it as weakening Indonesia's solidarity with Palestine, while supporters saw it as pragmatic diplomacy that could position Jakarta more strategically on the global stage.

The program also highlighted a wave of Palestinian recognition by Western nations, with Australia's Prime Minister Anthony Albanese explaining that his country's decision came after nearly 80 years of conflict, emphasizing that the 1948 UN decision originally called for two states. Trump's involvement added another layer, proposing a 20-point peace plan that includes ceasefire, hostage release, and technocratic Palestinian governance without Hamas involvement. However, critics dismissed Trump's plan as illegal and favoring Israel while providing minimal Palestinian representation.

## Implications

This coverage illustrates Indonesia's evolving role in Middle East diplomacy under Prabowo's leadership, potentially positioning the country as a bridge between competing interests while risking domestic backlash from traditional pro-Palestine constituencies. The growing Western recognition of Palestinian statehood, combined with persistent street protests, suggests shifting international dynamics that could pressure Israel despite continued US military support. The widespread criticism of UN ineffectiveness highlighted in the program reflects broader questions about global governance structures in addressing contemporary conflicts.
