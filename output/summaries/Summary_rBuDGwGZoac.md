## Overview

In this video, political commentator Akbar Faizal analyzes the acid attack case against investigative journalist Andrie Yunus, arguing that it bears strong political undertones similar to previous attacks on anti-corruption figures. Faizal draws parallels to the unsolved acid attack case against former KPK investigator Novel Baswedan, suggesting these incidents follow a pattern of politically motivated violence that authorities struggle to solve. The analysis emphasizes how cases with political dimensions tend to be systematically covered up, with perpetrators and masterminds remaining unidentified despite public attention.

## Key Highlights

Faizal establishes his central thesis by connecting the Andrie Yunus case to the earlier attack on Novel Baswedan, a prominent anti-corruption investigator. He argues that both cases share similar patterns of acid attacks targeting individuals involved in sensitive investigations or anti-corruption work. The host emphasizes that despite significant public attention and investigation, the Novel Baswedan case remains unsolved, with no definitive legal judgment identifying the actual perpetrators or the intellectual architects behind the attack. This persistent lack of resolution, according to Faizal, demonstrates a systematic problem in Indonesia's handling of politically sensitive criminal cases.

The commentary takes a particularly critical turn when Faizal notes that even Novel Baswedan himself has publicly suggested that the same perpetrators may not be behind both attacks. This observation leads Faizal to his key argument about the nature of politically motivated crimes in Indonesia. He contends that cases driven by motives beyond personal disputes—those with broader political implications—are systematically subjected to cover-ups that obstruct fact-finding and proper investigation. This pattern, he suggests, represents a deliberate effort to prevent the exposure of powerful actors who may be behind such attacks.

## Implications

This analysis highlights ongoing concerns about press freedom and the safety of journalists and anti-corruption advocates in Indonesia, suggesting that politically sensitive cases face institutional obstacles to proper resolution. The commentary underscores broader questions about rule of law and accountability when cases involve powerful political interests.
