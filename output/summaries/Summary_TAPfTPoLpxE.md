## Overview

This Tempo analysis examines Indonesia's measles outbreak affecting 14 provinces, with over 10,000 suspected cases and 4 deaths recorded through week 7 of 2025. The discussion explores multiple factors behind the surge, including delayed healthcare responses during COVID-19, vaccine supply issues, data accuracy problems, and reduced regional government budgets under President Prabowo that have affected health worker incentives. The analysis emphasizes that anti-vaccine sentiment, while present, is not the primary driver of the outbreak.

## Key Highlights

The measles outbreak represents a convergence of systemic healthcare challenges that began during the COVID-19 pandemic. Health experts identify several critical factors: healthcare workers' focus on COVID-19 treatment led to delays in routine measles immunization programs, creating gaps in herd immunity. Weak vaccination systems, compounded by misinformation spread through social media and messaging apps, have contributed to vaccine hesitancy among parents. The global trend of declining vaccine confidence, documented by UNICEF research from 2023, shows this is not uniquely an Indonesian problem but part of a worldwide phenomenon affecting vaccine trust post-COVID.

Data accuracy emerges as a persistent challenge, with epidemiologists reporting instances where immunization coverage was recorded as over 100% in some areas like Depok, raising questions about the reliability of health ministry statistics. Regional budget cuts implemented since President Prabowo took office have reduced incentives for health workers conducting door-to-door vaccination campaigns, particularly affecting remote and disaster-affected areas. The outbreak has disproportionately impacted areas like Sumenep in Madura, where 20 children died, and has spread to urban centers including Jakarta's Tambora district, which recorded nearly 2,000 suspected cases.

The government has responded with Outbreak Response Immunization (ORI) policies targeting priority areas and has extended vaccination permissions to adults following the death of a healthcare worker in Cianjur. However, implementation challenges persist, including vaccine supply shortages reported by pediatricians and logistical difficulties in maintaining cold chain storage, especially in disaster-affected regions.

## Implications

This outbreak highlights the fragility of Indonesia's public health infrastructure and the long-term consequences of pandemic-era policy decisions. The combination of budget cuts, data management issues, and vaccine supply problems suggests systemic reforms are needed to prevent future outbreaks and maintain public health security.
