## Overview

In this debate on the Akbar Faizal Uncensored channel, host Akbar Faizal engaged in a heated discussion with retired Lieutenant General Soleman Ponto, former head of Indonesia's military intelligence agency BAIS (2011-2013), regarding the acid attack case involving civilian activist Andrie Yunus by four military personnel. The central controversy revolves around whether such cases should be tried in military courts versus civilian courts, with both participants offering contrasting legal interpretations. The discussion exposed fundamental tensions between military and civilian justice systems in Indonesia, particularly when military personnel commit crimes against civilians.

## Key Highlights

The debate centered on the legal framework governing cases where military personnel commit crimes against civilians, specifically focusing on Article 170 of the Military Criminal Procedure Code regarding "connectivity" (koneksitas). Ponto argued that when only military personnel are perpetrators and civilians are victims, military courts maintain jurisdiction because existing laws only provide for civilian court jurisdiction when both military and civilian actors are co-perpetrators. He emphasized that there is no legal mechanism to transfer such cases to civilian courts when the perpetrator is solely military, regardless of the victim's civilian status. Faizal challenged this interpretation, arguing that Article 170's second paragraph, which considers where the "weight of harm" lies, should apply when civilian interests are more affected, thus warranting civilian court jurisdiction.

The conversation revealed deeper concerns about military culture and psychological conditioning that may contribute to such incidents. Ponto candidly acknowledged that military personnel are psychologically trained with a "kill or be killed" mentality and that violations by subordinates who failed in their duties (such as allowing protesters to disrupt the Fairmont Hotel meeting) could lead to vengeful behavior. He explained that the four perpetrators were allegedly motivated by revenge because they had been disciplinally punished (denied promotions and training opportunities) after civilian activists breached security during a TNI law discussion at the Fairmont Hotel. However, Faizal strongly criticized this explanation as oversimplified and dangerous, arguing that if military personnel can easily harbor grudges against civilians for performing their democratic rights, this poses a fundamental threat to civil-military relations.

The discussion highlighted systemic issues within Indonesia's legal framework regarding military accountability. Ponto suggested that a Presidential Regulation (Perppu) would be necessary to enable civilian court jurisdiction in cases involving military perpetrators and civilian victims, but noted that no such regulation has been enacted. Both participants agreed that the current legal framework is inadequate for addressing such cases, though they disagreed on the interpretation of existing provisions. The debate also touched on broader concerns about military personnel's psychological readiness and the need for better command oversight to prevent such incidents.

## Implications

This debate illuminates critical gaps in Indonesia's civil-military legal framework that leave civilian victims of military crimes potentially underserved by the justice system. The case has broader significance as it tests Indonesia's democratic institutions and the principle of civilian oversight of the military, particularly as the Constitutional Court is currently reviewing challenges to military court jurisdiction in such matters.
