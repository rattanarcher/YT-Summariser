## Overview
This video features Najwa Shihab's in-depth interview with Brandon Stanton, the American photographer and storyteller behind the globally acclaimed Humans of New York project. Stanton discusses his 15-year journey of capturing over 10,000 faces and stories on the streets of New York, his recent controversial series on Palestinian healthcare workers, and his latest artistic installation "Dear New York" at Grand Central Station. The conversation explores how simple human stories can create profound social impact, including generating millions in donations and bestselling books.

## Key Highlights

Stanton's journey began unconventionally after being fired from his job as a financial analyst in Chicago 15 years ago. With just a used camera and limited savings, he moved to New York with the simple goal of photographing 10,000 people. What started as random street photography evolved into a powerful storytelling platform that has reached hundreds of millions of readers worldwide. His approach centers on genuine human connection - approaching strangers and asking them to share their stories, proving that every ordinary person has extraordinary experiences worth telling.

The conversation delves deeply into Stanton's recent and most controversial work: a series featuring Palestinian healthcare workers with Doctors Without Borders. Unlike traditional journalism that approaches such topics with predetermined frameworks about conflict and genocide, Stanton's method focuses entirely on understanding individual human experiences. He emphasized that while discussing genocide and suffering naturally emerges from these conversations, his primary goal is always to understand each person's complete life experience - their loves, struggles, hopes, and agency in responding to their circumstances.

Stanton reflected on how his work has evolved from simple street photography into a mature artistic practice that balances showing human suffering without exploiting it. He stressed the importance of highlighting not just people's pain, but their agency and resilience in transforming that suffering into something meaningful. This philosophy proved particularly challenging with his Palestinian series, where the suffering often seemed senseless and inescapable, yet he still found extraordinary examples of human strength and compassion among his subjects.

## Implications

This interview reveals how independent digital storytelling can create significant social impact outside traditional media structures, demonstrating the power of authentic human connection in an increasingly polarized world. Stanton's approach to controversial subjects like the Palestinian conflict offers insights into alternative journalism methods that prioritize individual human experiences over political narratives, potentially influencing how sensitive topics are covered in Indonesian and global media.
