## Overview

This Mata Najwa episode profiles Brandon Stanton, the creator of the globally renowned "Humans of New York" project, during his recent visit to Indonesia. Over 15 years, Stanton has photographed and collected stories from more than 10,000 people, transforming simple street encounters into a multimedia phenomenon that has generated bestselling books, millions of followers, and substantial charitable donations. The interview explores his methodology, recent controversial work documenting Palestinian medical workers, and his latest project "Dear New York" - a massive art installation at Grand Central Station that serves as both a love letter to the city and a culmination of his artistic journey.

## Key Highlights

Stanton's approach to storytelling fundamentally differs from traditional journalism through his emphasis on human connection over predetermined narratives. When documenting sensitive subjects like Palestinian healthcare workers or Syrian refugees, he deliberately avoids arriving with a fixed framework about genocide or conflict. Instead, he tells each subject that he's there to learn about their life and experiences, allowing more profound themes to emerge organically. This methodology has enabled him to capture deeply personal stories that resonate universally, from immigrant families whose businesses received million-dollar donations after being featured, to individuals who found their voices amplified to global audiences. His recent Palestine series, created in collaboration with Doctors Without Borders, exemplifies this approach by focusing on individual resilience and agency rather than victimization, even within the context of immense suffering.

The "Dear New York" project represents the culmination of Stanton's artistic evolution and his relationship with the city that transformed his life. After being fired from his job as a financial analyst in Chicago 15 years ago, he moved to New York with just a used camera and modest savings, initially planning to photograph 10,000 people. The recent Grand Central installation featured 150 digital screens and transformed the entire station into an immersive art gallery displaying hundreds of portraits and stories collected over his career. Stanton invested all proceeds from his accompanying book into the installation, creating what he describes as a "cathedral to humanity" and a complete artistic statement about New York as a microcosm of all human experience.

## Implications

Stanton's work demonstrates the power of digital platforms to democratize storytelling and create social impact, with individual stories generating millions in donations and global awareness for various causes. His approach to covering controversial topics like the Israeli-Palestinian conflict through personal narratives rather than political frameworks offers a model for more nuanced journalism in polarized times, though it also highlights the challenges content creators face when addressing sensitive geopolitical issues on social media platforms.
