## Overview
This episode of Musyawarah, hosted by Najwa Shihab and Jovi Adhiguna, covers several serious topics including Najwa's recent coverage of pro-Palestine demonstrations in New York during the UN General Assembly, problems with Indonesia's Free Nutritious Meals (MBG) program including food poisoning incidents, the tragic collapse of a boarding school building in Sidoarjo that killed dozens of students, and a brief interview with Professor Matthew Nicholson about Monash University Indonesia's expansion. The episode concludes with Jovi addressing his recent breakup after a six-year relationship.

## Key Highlights

The episode opens with Najwa describing her week-long coverage in New York, where she chose to report on massive pro-Palestine demonstrations rather than cover Netanyahu's UN speech. She documented protests involving up to 20,000 people, interviewing activists who emphasized that their movement represents the true face of America, contrary to what political leaders suggest. Najwa contextualizes the current situation by noting that Palestinian suffering has lasted 77 years since the 1948 Nakba, not just the two years since October 7th, 2023. She describes this as a global awakening of conscience, with similar massive demonstrations occurring in Amsterdam (250,000 people), Australia (over 100,000), and across Europe.

The discussion shifts to Indonesia's Free Nutritious Meals program, which has faced numerous challenges including food poisoning incidents affecting nearly 6,000 children across multiple cities. While acknowledging the program's good intentions to ensure nutritious meals for Indonesian children, both hosts critique the rushed implementation and inadequate safety standards. They compare Indonesia's approach to successful programs in Brazil and South Korea, which took 40 and 66 years respectively to perfect their systems. These countries succeeded through decentralized management, community participation, local sourcing from farmers, and integration with educational curricula, demonstrating that such programs should be comprehensive movements rather than mere feeding initiatives.

The most somber topic discussed is the collapse of a boarding school prayer hall in Sidoarjo, East Java, which killed 67-68 students during afternoon prayers. The hosts express frustration with the school administrator's attribution of the tragedy to "fate," arguing that proper investigation is necessary to prevent future incidents. They highlight systemic issues including lack of proper building permits, inadequate oversight, and potentially substandard construction practices involving student labor. The discussion emphasizes that building safety requires human responsibility and professional oversight, not just religious acceptance of tragedy.

## Implications

These discussions highlight ongoing challenges in Indonesian governance and social policy, from international relations positioning on Palestine to domestic program implementation and regulatory oversight. The MBG program's struggles demonstrate the difficulty of scaling large social programs without proper systems and community involvement, while the building collapse reveals gaps in construction oversight that could affect many other educational institutions across the country.
