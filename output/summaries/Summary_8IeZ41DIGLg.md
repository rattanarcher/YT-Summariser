## Overview
This video features prominent Indonesian documentary filmmaker and journalist Dandhy Dwi Laksono discussing his latest film "Pesta Babi" (Pig Party), which examines alleged collusion between military forces and business conglomerates in Papua's resource extraction projects. Laksono argues that government narratives about food security and energy independence are covers for large-scale land grabbing that displaces indigenous communities. The conversation explores broader themes about Indonesia's political discourse, media freedom, and the challenges facing critical journalism under current regulations like the ITE Law.

## Key Highlights

Laksono presents a damning critique of Indonesia's approach to development in Papua, arguing that the government's stated goals of food and energy self-sufficiency are mere pretexts for resource extraction that benefits corporate interests while harming indigenous communities. He contends that military deployment in Papua - with one soldier for every 100 Papuans compared to one for every 690 Indonesians nationally - serves not to combat separatists but to secure land for bio-energy projects. The documentary filmmaker traces this as the seventh wave of resource exploitation in Papua's history, following oil and gas, timber, coal, palm oil, and nickel extraction.

The discussion reveals deeper concerns about Indonesia's democratic trajectory, with Laksono arguing that the country is experiencing a regression toward authoritarianism disguised as "guided capitalism." He criticizes the government's evidence-free policymaking, citing failures like food estate projects that have never succeeded anywhere in the world, yet continue to be expanded on massive scales. The conversation touches on how digital technology and AI are complicating the media landscape, potentially undermining the power of visual documentation that has historically exposed government wrongdoing, such as the Santa Cruz massacre footage that changed international opinion on East Timor.

## Implications
This discussion highlights growing tensions between Indonesia's critical media voices and government authorities, particularly regarding resource extraction in Papua and transparency in policymaking. The interview underscores concerns about democratic backsliding and the shrinking space for legitimate criticism of government policies, with potential implications for Indonesia's international reputation and internal stability.
