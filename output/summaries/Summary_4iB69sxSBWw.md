## Overview
This video features a heated roundtable debate in Makassar about Indonesia's new state-owned enterprise (SOE/BUMN) management model under Danantara, a superholding entity established in 2025. The discussion, hosted by political commentator Akbar Faisal as part of Nagara Institute's national tour, brings together academics and policy experts to debate the pros and cons of this major economic transformation. The debate reveals deep divisions over whether consolidating over 1,000 SOEs under Danantara represents a necessary reform or a risky political gamble.

## Key Highlights

The discussion centers on the controversial transformation of Indonesia's state-owned enterprises under President Prabowo's administration, which consolidates 1,056 SOEs into 285 entities under the Danantara superholding structure. Proponents like economist Wijayanto Samirin argue this represents the fulfillment of a decades-old vision for "Indonesia Incorporated," comparing it to successful sovereign wealth funds like Singapore's Temasek and Malaysia's Khazanah. They contend that the new structure provides necessary flexibility for SOE management and follows international best practices, with proper regulatory frameworks to prevent political interference. Supporters emphasize that the previous SOE management system was fundamentally broken, with return on assets below 3 percent and widespread inefficiencies costing 30 trillion rupiah annually.

However, critics led by economist Awalil Rizki present damning financial analysis showing that Indonesian SOEs are essentially "permanently failing organizations" - healthy enough to be exploited by politicians but never allowed to completely fail. They argue that Danantara's debt-to-equity ratio of 198 percent indicates severe financial distress, and question how an entity managing predominantly sick assets can generate the promised returns. Critics also raise concerns about governance structures, noting that Danantara operates without clear oversight mechanisms and lacks transparency about its investment strategy and timeline for returning dividends to the state budget.

The debate also explores regional economic impact, with researchers presenting eight models for how SOE transformation could benefit local economies, from place-based impact investing to regional industrial clusters. However, participants from various Indonesian cities consistently ask what tangible benefits their regions receive from SOE presence, highlighting the disconnect between national SOE policy and regional economic development needs.

## Implications

This debate reflects broader tensions in Indonesian economic policy between technocratic reform ambitions and political economy realities. The success or failure of Danantara will significantly impact Indonesia's fiscal position and the government's ability to achieve its 8 percent GDP growth target, making this transformation one of the most consequential economic policy decisions in recent Indonesian history.
